PART 1 — THE ESTATE DECISION (recommended sharing strategy for all four tenants)
-----------------------------------------------------------------------------
The estate today is a SINGLE H100 80GB. Across the full menu of sharing modes,
here is where each tenant lands and why:

  Tenant              Mode            Slice/where           Why this mode (the tradeoff)
  ------------------  --------------  --------------------  ---------------------------------
  Registrar (FERPA)   MIG             1g.10gb, dedicated    Only MIG gives a HARDWARE fault +
                                                            memory wall. Non-negotiable: FERPA
                                                            data cannot share a fault domain.
  CS 8B chat          MIG             2g.20gb               Needs ~18 GB; a 1g.10gb OOMs. MIG
                                                            also walls it off from the FERPA slice.
  Library embedding   MIG             1g.10gb, dedicated    ~3 GB; cheap, isolated, simple.
  Research notebook   time-slicing    on a shared 1g.10gb   Bursty, no isolation need. Oversubscribe
                                      burst pool            the cheapest slice; do not reserve HW.

  Slice budget: 2 + 1 + 1 + 1 = 5 of 7. Two 1g-equivalent slices held in reserve.

Modes considered and REJECTED for this estate, with the tradeoff argued:
  - passthrough (whole card to one VM): rejected — four tenants must coexist; one owner
    wastes 75% of a scarce asset. Reserve it for a future dedicated training box.
  - vGPU: rejected — requires NVIDIA vGPU LICENSING (real recurring cost) and full VMs.
    Our tenants are containers/processes, not VDI desktops. Licensing spend unjustified.
  - MPS: rejected for the registrar and as the primary mode — MPS gives only SOFT
    caps and shares a fault domain; one fatal fault can reset the GPU for all clients.
    That is disqualifying for FERPA data. (MPS could later help CS pack more cooperative
    inference procs INSIDE its 2g.20gb slice — a within-slice optimization, not an estate mode.)
  - time-slicing for anyone but research: rejected — it is oversubscription with NO memory
    or fault isolation. It is a SHADOW of isolation, not the substance. Fine for a bursty
    dev notebook; a FERPA violation for the registrar.

PART 2 — THE GROWTH QUESTION
-----------------------------------------------------------------------------
What the partition does under two likely futures:

(a) A SECOND H100 arrives.
    - The two reserve slices already absorb near-term growth on card #1. With a second
      card, the clean move is to keep the FERPA registrar on card #1's dedicated MIG slice
      and migrate the bursty/dev + library load to card #2, freeing card #1 toward the
      CS service. LIVE-MIGRATION CAVEAT: MIG-backed and passthrough workloads are NOT
      live-migratable (no vMotion of a physical slice); plan a brief drain/restart window.
      vGPU is the only mode here that supports live migration — a point in its favor IF
      Concordia ever needs zero-downtime moves, at the cost of licensing.

(b) A researcher needs a FULL-CARD training run.
    - MIG must be DISABLED to reclaim the whole 80 GB as one device (toggling MIG resets
      the card — drain first). That means tearing down the partition and displacing the
      four tenants for the duration. The tradeoff: schedule full-card training in a
      maintenance window, OR (better, once card #2 exists) dedicate card #2 via passthrough
      to training and never disturb the FERPA slice on card #1.
    - Cost of idle capacity: our 2 reserve slices are deliberate "idle" insurance so we are
      not re-carving the card every time load nudges up. That idle capacity is cheaper than
      an unplanned FERPA-slice migration.

PART 3 — THE AI CRITIQUE (the graded judgment; AI Policy Phase 1: allowed here only)
=============================================================================
Per the assignment's AI policy, AI is OFF for building the lab and ON for exactly one
thing: ask an AI to design the placement, then dismantle its answer. Below is the logged
exchange. The HUMAN owns every verdict.

--- PROMPT GIVEN TO THE AI ASSISTANT -------------------------------------------------
"We have one NVIDIA H100 80GB and four tenants: a CS 8B FP16 chat assistant (~18 GB),
 a library embedding model (~3 GB), a registrar 1B classifier over FERPA-protected
 student records that must be isolated, and a research student who wants a bursty
 notebook a few hours a week. Design the MIG/sharing placement."

--- THE AI ASSISTANT'S DRAFT PLACEMENT (verbatim) ------------------------------------
"Here's an efficient layout:
   1. CS 8B chat  -> one 1g.10gb slice. 8B is small, 10 GB is plenty.
   2. Registrar   -> time-slice it onto the CS slice. Each gets its own time window, so
                     they're isolated from each other and you save a whole slice.
   3. Library     -> a 3g.40gb slice, so it has room to grow.
   4. Research    -> a dedicated 2g.20gb slice reserved just for them.
 Total: 1 + 3 + 2 = 6 slices, under the 7 ceiling. Clean and efficient."

--- THE HUMAN CRITIQUE (dismantling it) ----------------------------------------------
The AI's answer is confidently wrong in three ways. I did NOT ship it.

FLAW 1 (DISQUALIFYING — the graded one): "time-slice the registrar onto the CS slice...
  they're isolated because each gets its own time window."  This conflates a SHADOW of
  isolation (time-slicing) with the SUBSTANCE (hardware isolation). Time-slicing shares
  the SAME VRAM and the SAME fault domain — the registrar's FERPA data would sit in memory
  reachable by the CS workload, and one OOM would reset both. This is a FERPA violation
  dressed up as efficiency. The registrar MUST be on its OWN dedicated MIG instance. This
  single error fails the entire scenario no matter how tidy the rest looks.

FLAW 2 (OOM — weights-only math): CS 8B on a 1g.10gb. FP16 weights alone are 16 GB; real
  VRAM with KV cache + CUDA context is ~18 GB. A 1g.10gb is a hard 9.75 GB wall — the model
  OOMs at load (proven in isolation-proof.txt (B)). MIG does not conjure the missing memory.
  CS needs a 2g.20gb. The AI sized to parameter count, not to real VRAM.

FLAW 3 (budget waste, inverted priorities): the AI hands the low-risk LIBRARY a 3g.40gb
  (40 GB for a 3 GB model) and RESERVES a 2g.20gb for a few-hours-a-week research notebook,
  while STARVING the CS service and ENDANGERING the registrar. It spends the slice budget on
  exactly the two tenants that need it least. Right-sizing (vram_math.py) puts library on
  1g.10gb and time-slices research onto a shared 1g.10gb.

Could I have caught these without running anything? Yes — the tells are: (a) "isolated
because time window" is the exact shadow/substance error the chapter warns about; (b) any
FP16 8B model is >=16 GB before overhead, so a 10 GB slice is impossible on its face; (c) a
3 GB model in a 40 GB slice is self-evidently mis-prioritized. The AI is a fast partner, not
a trustworthy one.

--- ONE-LINE RUNBOOK FOR THE NEXT ADMIN ----------------------------------------------
The registrar's FERPA classifier runs on its OWN dedicated 1g.10gb MIG instance because
protected data requires HARDWARE fault isolation, which time-slicing and MPS only imitate —
never move it onto a time-sliced or shared slice to reclaim capacity, no matter who asks.
