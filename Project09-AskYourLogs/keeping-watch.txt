THE INCIDENT IN ONE LINE
------------------------
For about 90 seconds starting at 14:05, checkout aborted for three customers
because the upstream payment provider (stripe) timed out (HTTP 504, ~5s
latency). Checkout was the SYMPTOM; the payment-provider timeout was the ROOT
CAUSE. The system recovered on its own by 14:07.

Correlation signal: trace_id. Each "checkout aborted" line shares its trace_id
with a "payments authorize failed" line, which is how we prove the checkout
failures were downstream of the payment timeouts and not an independent bug.

Affected users: u-4419 (d4e1), u-4420 (e2a8), u-4422 (aa01).

WOULD I HAVE SHIPPED A FIX ON THE AI'S ANSWER ALONE?
-----------------------------------------------------
No. The AI is a fast intern that finds relevant lines quickly, and for Q1/Q2
its filter overlapped with mine. But for the question that actually matters —
what do we fix? — the AI's filter pointed at the checkout errors, which is the
symptom. Shipping a "checkout fix" would have wasted a sprint while the real
fault (payment-provider timeout / missing retry + timeout budget) stayed live.
I verified every generated filter against the raw lines and used trace_id
correlation to reach the root cause myself. That verification step is the job.


WHAT I WOULD ACTUALLY DO NEXT (the fix the evidence points to)
--------------------------------------------------------------
- Treat the payment provider as the failure domain: add a sane timeout budget
  and a retry/circuit-breaker around the stripe authorize call, and alert on
  provider latency (the 14:04 WARN "provider latency high 2710ms" was the early
  warning we ignored).
- Alert on the SYMPTOM's burn rate (checkout error ratio), not on a raw error
  count, so one blip does not page anyone but a real budget-burning event does.
- Keep the trace_id on every line so the next on-call can ask instead of grep.
