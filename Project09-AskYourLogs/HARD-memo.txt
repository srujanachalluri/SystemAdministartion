We now have observability that lets on-call ASK the logs instead of grepping
them at midnight. The next question is how much we let an AIOps agent DO on its
own. Here is my recommendation. It is a ladder, not a switch.

THE AUTONOMY LADDER
-------------------
1. ACT AUTONOMOUSLY (read-only, reversible):
   - Summarize an alert episode, cluster duplicate alerts into one incident,
     and correlate by trace_id (exactly the 14:05 case: three "checkout"
     failures were one payment-provider timeout).
   - Draft the incident timeline and propose the likely root cause.
   These are safe because they change nothing. Worst case is a wrong summary,
   which a human reads before acting.

2. ACT WITH HUMAN APPROVAL (state-changing but bounded):
   - Restart a stuck service, drain a node, roll back a deploy, open a ticket
     to the payment provider, flip a feature flag.
   The agent may PREPARE and RECOMMEND these; a human clicks the button. A
   confidently wrong agent that restarts the wrong service turns a 90-second
   blip into an outage.

3. NEVER UNATTENDED (expensive or hard to reverse):
   - Scaling down the GPU inference node, changing the payment provider config,
     or anything that touches money, customer data, or the most expensive box
     in the rack. The GPU at 97% utilization with KV-cache pressure is not
     automatically "idle" — pair it with TTFT and queue depth before ANY
     scale decision, and keep that decision human.

WHY THIS LINE
-------------
The 14:05 incident is the argument. An agent doing a fast first pass is
genuinely useful — it found the 14:05 error lines in seconds. But the filter it
generates cannot correlate across services or separate cause from symptom; it
pointed at checkout (the symptom) and had no signal that payments (the cause)
timed out upstream. If we had let it "fix" what it flagged, we would have spent
a sprint on checkout while the real fault stayed live. The agent is a very fast
intern. We supervise interns.

FINOPS NOTE
-----------
Autonomy has a bill. When an agent investigates an alert storm, each delegated
step is tokens, and vendor SRE-agent pricing in 2026 (Datadog Bits AI, New
Relic, Azure SRE Agent's AAU/AAC billing) charges per action/investigation —
so an alert storm that fans out into hundreds of agent calls can run up spend
faster than the incident itself costs. Two controls: (1) cap agent actions per
incident and per hour, and (2) keep the expensive, irreversible actions (GPU
scale-down, provider changes) behind a human, where the cost of being wrong is
highest. Cheap-and-reversible: let it run. Expensive-or-irreversible: it may
recommend, a human decides.

RECOMMENDATION
--------------
Adopt the three-rung ladder above now: autonomous read-only triage, approval
for bounded state changes, and a hard human gate on GPU/payment/cost actions.
The agent may draft the recommendation on any incident — but a human approves
the state-changing ones, and I am accountable for that line. That is the job,
and I am standing behind it.
