AI policy for this chapter (Phase 1): an AI copilot is allowed, but the
traditional analysis must be done first/also, and this file must honestly state
what was delegated and what was verified by hand. The human owns every verdict
in the report.

WHAT I DELEGATED TO AN AI COPILOT
---------------------------------
- Scaffolding of watchman.py (function structure, argument parsing, byte-
  formatting helper) starting from the instructor's flow_baseline.py reference.
- Wording help on REPORT.docx and MEMO.docx, especially compressing the memo
  into plain language a non-engineer director can act on.
- Drafting of this README/AI_USAGE structure.

WHAT I DID AND VERIFIED BY HAND
-------------------------------
- I read window_flows.csv and baseline_flows.csv line by line myself and
  identified the three anomalies by eye BEFORE trusting any detector:
    * 10.20.4.62 -> 203.0.113.77:8443 repeating every 5 minutes (beaconing)
    * 10.20.4.77 hitting six hosts on ports 22/3389/445 in one second (scan)
    * 10.20.4.45 sending 1,887,436,800 bytes to 198.51.100.42 (exfil)
- I confirmed each detector finding against the raw CSV rows, and checked the
  math on the baseline profile (p95 outbound, known destinations, known ports)
  by hand for all four hosts.
- I decided the false-positive reasoning myself: the git-pull and package-mirror
  flows are periodic and public but appear in the host's own baseline, so they
  are behaviorally known-good. I did NOT suppress them by special-casing IPs.
- I made the final verdict and the recommended order of response in the report
  and memo. The AI did not decide what is malicious; I did.

WHAT I EXPLICITLY DID NOT LET THE AI DO
---------------------------------------
- I did not accept any flag the AI could not tie back to a concrete baseline
  deviation I could see in the data.
- I did not let the AI pick detection thresholds without me checking that each
  one still made sense against this specific host baseline.
