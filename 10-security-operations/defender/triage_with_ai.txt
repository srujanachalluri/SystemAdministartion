defender/triage_with_ai.txt — AI SOC assistant vs. deterministic detector
Project 10, Normal Tier requirement #4.

======================================================================
PROMPT GIVEN TO THE AI ASSISTANT
======================================================================
"You are a SOC analyst assistant. Here is a raw auth.log for a small
ministry (format: timestamp host service user srcip geo result note).
Write a short incident summary: what happened, who was involved, and
what you would recommend. Do not just repeat rows back — draw
conclusions."
[full contents of code/auth.log pasted below the prompt]

======================================================================
AI ASSISTANT'S RAW OUTPUT (captured verbatim, not edited)
======================================================================
"Incident summary: User jvargas's account was compromised starting
around 13:22 UTC on 2026-06-15. The attacker's first sign was an
impossible-travel failure from Romania (203.0.113.55), followed by a
password-spray burst, then a successful login after what looks like
MFA push-bombing at 13:23:14. The attacker escalated privileges on
app1 via sudo with no change ticket, then moved laterally to db1 and
pulled a large batch of records from the donors table before sending
data out to 203.0.113.55 over HTTPS (curl to port 8443) — this looks
like exfiltration.

I also flag the svc_bkp service-account login at 11:05:31 as likely
compromised — it's unusual for a service account to authenticate in
the middle of this incident window, and I'd disable it as a
precaution.

Recommendation: revoke jvargas's sessions, force a passkey
re-enrollment, block 203.0.113.55, and disable svc_bkp pending
review."

======================================================================
GRADING — deterministic detect.py vs. the AI's summary
======================================================================
Graded like a TA grades a student: "helpful" is not a grade. Specific
claims checked against the raw log and against defender/detect.py.

WHAT THE AI GOT RIGHT
- Correctly identified jvargas as the compromised account and 13:22 UTC
  as the start of the incident.
- Correctly named impossible travel, password spray, and MFA
  fatigue/push-bombing in the right order — this matches detect.py's
  Rule 7 output exactly.
- Correctly identified the sudo-with-no-change-ticket step (matches
  Rule 4) and the lateral move to db1.
- Correctly identified the bulk donor-table read AND the curl
  exfiltration to 203.0.113.55:8443 as the end of the chain. This is
  the same step detect.py's new Rule 6 was written to catch — the
  step the ORIGINAL baseline (code/triage_logins.py) missed. The AI
  caught it; the hand-written baseline didn't. Score this honestly:
  the AI added real value here, not just noise.

WHAT THE AI GOT WRONG / INVENTED
- The AI flagged svc_bkp (11:05:31) as "likely compromised." The raw
  log contradicts this: that row is result=ok, geo=US-IL (home
  geography), and the note explicitly says "service account,
  scheduled." It is 2+ hours before the attack chain even starts and
  has no relationship to jvargas, app1, db1, or the 203.0.113.55
  source IP. This is a hallucinated finding — the AI pattern-matched
  "service account" + "unusual" without checking the fields that
  would have ruled it out. detect.py does NOT flag this row, and it
  should not be flagged.
- The AI's recommendation to "disable svc_bkp pending review" is an
  action based on that hallucinated finding. Acting on it would have
  broken a legitimate scheduled backup job for no security benefit —
  exactly the kind of unforced error a human reviewer has to catch
  before a recommendation like this reaches an automated playbook
  (see code/soar_playbook.yml, disable_account requires
  approval_gate for this reason).

WHAT THE DETERMINISTIC RULES CAUGHT THAT THE AI DID NOT
- Nothing material — the AI's coverage of the real chain was
  actually equal to or better than detect.py's on this log. The
  gap ran the other direction (see above).

VERDICT
The AI assistant is a useful triage accelerant — it found the same
9-step chain a human built by hand, faster, and it caught the exact
step (bulk donor read) that the hand-written baseline detector
originally missed. But it also fabricated a finding (svc_bkp) that a
careless analyst could have acted on. This is exactly why the SOAR
playbook (code/soar_playbook.yml) never lets an AI step act with
autonomy: recommend on its own, and why every high-impact action
requires a human approval_gate. The AI is a capable junior analyst,
not the analyst who signs the incident report.
